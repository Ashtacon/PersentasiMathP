The Derivative of Trigonometric Functions is a concept in calculus that deals with calculating the derivatives of trigonometric functions. Derivatives measure the rate of change of a function with respect to its independent variable.

Derivative Formulas for
Trigonometric Functions

Derivative of sin(x): d/dx(sin(x)) = cos(x)
Derivative of cos(x): d/dx(cos(x)) = -sin(x)
Derivative of tan(x): d/dx(tan(x)) = sec^2(x)
Derivative of csc(x): d/dx(csc(x)) = -csc(x) cot(x)
Derivative of sec(x): d/dx(sec(x)) = sec(x) tan(x)
Derivative of cot(x): d/dx(cot(x)) = -csc^2(x)

Example

A man is riding a motorcycle along a curved road with the equation "y = sin(x)," where "x" is the angle in radians, and "y" is the height of the road above the surface. If he is moving at a constant speed of "v = 40" km/h, calculate the rate of change of height with respect to the angle when he is at "π/6" radians.

To calculate the rate of change of height with respect to the angle (dy/dx), we need to find the derivative of the function y = sin(x). The derivative of sin(x) is cos(x). So, dy/dx = cos(π/6) = √3/2. Now, we can calculate the rate of change of height using the following formula: Rate of Height Change = (dy/dx) × Velocity = (√3/2) × 40 km/h.
Answer to Story Problem: The rate of change of height when he is at π/6 radians is 20√3 km/h.
